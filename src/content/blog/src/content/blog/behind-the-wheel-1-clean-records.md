*Behind the Wheel — what I've learned from the road, the dock, and the dispatch desk.*

Most people picture freight fraud the way you'd picture any scam — something that *looks* wrong. A sketchy operation, a rate that's too good, a name nobody's heard of. The instinct is that if you check authority and insurance and the carrier comes back clean, you're safe.

That instinct is now wrong, and there's a number that proves it: in the first quarter of 2026, half of all freight fraud was committed by carriers with clean records. Not new operations. Not obvious red flags. Carriers with years of history, active insurance, and a track record that passes exactly the checks most people rely on.

Here's the mechanism underneath that, because it's the part nobody explains — and once you see it, you can't unsee it.

There's a growing fraud where the thief doesn't build a fake carrier. They buy a real one. An established motor carrier — clean safety record, years of operating history, real insurance, real relationships — gets sold. Sometimes the original owner thinks they're selling to a legitimate buyer. But what actually changes hands isn't just a company. It's an entire identity: the authority, the phone number, the email, the bank account, sometimes even a copy of the former owner's license. The buyer now *is* that carrier, on paper and in every system that checks paper.

And then they go to work stealing freight — under a record that's still spotless, because the record belonged to someone who did nothing wrong.

This is why the old screen doesn't catch it. Checking authority and insurance confirms the *credentials* are legitimate. It cannot tell you the person holding those credentials today is the same person who earned them. Those used to be the same question. They aren't anymore — and the gap between them can run for weeks before anyone notices. In one documented case, eight separate cargo-theft claims were filed before the insurance company finally reached the original carrier — who had no idea his identity was still out there moving loads.

Sit with that. A real company, a clean operator, a good record — and his name was on eight thefts before anyone told him he'd been robbed too.

So here's what actually happens before a carrier moves a load for us. It is not one check at signup. It is a standard that runs the whole way through:

- We verify who they are — not just that valid authority exists, but that the company contacting us is the one that holds it.
- We confirm the person we're dealing with is actually authorized to act for that carrier — because the threat isn't a fake company, it's a real one in the wrong hands.
- We watch for the signals that an established identity may have changed underneath us — a dormant number suddenly active again, contact details that shifted, a new urgency around payment or dispatch that wasn't there before.
- And we confirm, at the moment freight moves, that we're handing it to the company we actually intended to hire — not just to a clean MC number.

None of that is visible to you. It happens before your load is ever assigned, and when it works, you never know it happened. The freight just shows up where it's supposed to, with the carrier we vetted, and nothing goes wrong. That's the tell of good freight handling — not the saves you hear about, but the problems that never reach you.

The reason I'm putting this out there: the threat changed, so the standard had to change. "Is this carrier real" was the right question for a long time. It isn't the whole question anymore, because the most dangerous fraud in freight right now is built entirely out of *real* — real authority, real insurance, real history, in hands that stole all three.

A clean record tells you a company earned trust. It no longer tells you who's driving away with your freight.
